Speculative Is the New Portfolio: Building Brands With No Ceiling

The Ninth · WISP · LevelConcept · brand systems · AIMay 24, 20265 min read

Alongside paid work, I build complete brand systems for companies that don't exist. An esports membership product, an AI host with no face, a prediction market that protects the people who use it. No client briefed them, no budget approved them, no committee trimmed them. I build them because speculative work has quietly become one of the most useful things a creative director can do, especially now that the tools make a full build cheap and the only scarce thing left is whether the thinking holds.

A concept proves range a real case study cannot.

Client work is bounded by what the client needed, what the budget allowed, and what the committee would approve, and a viewer never sees the shape those constraints carved. A speculative concept has none of those ceilings, so it lets me design a full system end to end, brand, product UI, motion, social, and a working AI layer, and show the cross-channel thinking with nothing watered down in a review. Anyone who has shipped real work knows the gap between the version in the first deck and the version that survived three rounds of stakeholder notes, and a portfolio mostly shows you the survivors. For someone hiring a creative leader, a concept built whole is often a clearer signal of how a person actually thinks than a polished real project, because the real project is partly a record of compromises that had nothing to do with judgment. The concept is all judgment, which is exactly the thing being hired, and it is the one thing the constrained work cannot fully show.

Speculative work is how I take a position before the brief arrives.

Each concept starts from an industry that is large now or rising fast, and treats AI two ways on purpose. There is a functional AI layer the user actually touches, and an AI-accelerated pipeline that builds the work, and neither is decoration. Mobile esports viewership and revenue have been climbing hard into 2025 by every industry tracker, which is exactly why The Ninth starts there rather than from a mood. The point is not to predict the future, which is a fool's errand, it is to have already done the thinking when a slice of that future shows up as a paid brief. Designing for that world before a client asks means that by the time the brief lands I have already worked through the product it lives in, the failure modes, the tone, and the trust problems nobody on the kickoff call has named yet. Speculative work is how I stay ahead of the curve instead of reacting to it a quarter late, which is the difference between leading a category conversation and catching up to it.

A client project shows what I did. A speculative system shows what I would do if nobody told me where to stop.

The constraint you never drop is honesty.

Speculative does not mean fictional in the dishonest sense, and the line is bright. Every market figure in these concepts is sourced and describes the real industry, never a result I am claiming, and every projected outcome is labeled an objective rather than a win. When Level cites a documented harm problem in its category, that is a real, sourced figure about the world, not a number the concept invented to flatter itself. This is the rule that separates a concept worth showing from a fantasy, because the temptation is always to write the success story into the deck, the made-up retention curve and the imaginary conversion lift that make the work look proven. The moment a speculative system starts quoting its own invented success it stops being a credible argument and becomes a mood board with delusions, so the discipline is to design ambitiously and stay ruthless about what is actually real on the page, which is the same honesty rule I hold the paid work to.

In the AI era this matters more, because looking real is now free.

Gen AI has made it cheap to produce a full, convincing speculative build, which means the bar is no longer whether you can make it look real, it is whether the thinking holds when someone pushes on it. A point of view is the moat. The concepts that matter are the ones with a genuine argument about a hard problem, designed all the way through to the edges, and in a field where execution is commoditizing fast, putting something complete and opinionated in front of the world that nobody asked you to make is one of the strongest moves available. A few years ago a polished speculative build was itself the achievement, proof you could execute at a high level, and that proof has quietly evaporated now that the tools hand everyone the polish. What is left to judge is the argument underneath, the opinion the work is willing to defend. It says what you would do if no one told you where to stop, which is the one thing a client brief can never show, and the one thing worth hiring for.

FAQ

How do you pick which speculative concept to build next?+
I look for an industry that is both large or fast-rising and genuinely unsolved on the brand and trust side, because that is where a point of view is worth the most. The category has to be real enough that the thinking transfers to a future brief, and broken enough that there is something honest to argue. If a concept could only ever be a styling exercise, it does not make the cut.
Don't speculative projects risk looking unhireable, like you only do dream work?+
That is why they live next to the paid case studies, not instead of them. The concepts show the ceiling of the thinking, and the client work shows it surviving real constraints and still moving a number, and a hiring manager needs both halves to trust either one. On its own, a wall of concepts reads as a stylist. Paired with shipped results, it reads as range with receipts.
Can speculative work actually lead to paid work, or is it just a showcase?+
It does both, and the lead-generation part is the underrated half. A concept that takes a clear position on where a category is going is a conversation starter with exactly the people building in that category, far better than a generic portfolio. It signals what I would do before they have spent a dollar finding out, which is a much cheaper way for both sides to learn if there is a fit.
