Audience-First, Org-Second: The Order That Fixes Most Brand and Product Work

DealNews · SPORTIMEBrand · product · methodApril 4, 20266 min read

Most brand and product work starts in the wrong place. It starts with the organization: the goal, the launch date, the thing the company needs people to do. The audience gets added later, as a target to hit rather than a person to understand. Flip the order and most of the hard decisions get easier, because you're no longer guessing.

Start with what the person already knows

Before I touch a logo, a screen, or a campaign, I write down what the person on the other end actually knows, needs, and responds to. Not a persona with a stock photo and a fake name. The real mental state they arrive in. A Cloud9 fan doesn't want another hoodie, they want a reason to show up on a Tuesday with no match on. A first-time prediction-market user doesn't want a trading terminal, they want to understand a probability without feeling hustled. Get that right and the brief writes half of itself.

When I rebuilt the DealNews app for 17M+ monthly users, the wins didn't come from a prettier interface. They came from designing around what a deal-hunter is actually doing in the three seconds before they bounce. The 18% conversion lift and the climb from a 3.8 to a 4.6 rating were the audience telling us we'd finally understood them.

The org's goals get met on the way, not at the start

This is the part that makes executives nervous, so let me be precise. Audience-first doesn't mean ignoring the business. It means the business goal is the destination, and the audience is the road. At SPORTIME, the org needed the new Pickleball vertical to fill 200+ courts. The fastest way there wasn't to talk about the courts. It was to give a specific kind of player a place to belong, and let the court bookings follow.

Lead with the org and you get work that explains itself. Lead with the audience and you get work that earns attention.

It is a forcing function, not a philosophy

Audience-first is useful because it kills bad ideas early. Every feature, every headline, every animation gets the same test: does this serve what the person actually knows and responds to, or does it serve our internal comfort? Internal-comfort decisions are easy to spot once you're looking. The jargon nobody outside the building uses. The feature that exists because a stakeholder wanted it. The launch sequence built around the company calendar instead of the customer's.

Why this matters more now

As AI floods every category with competent, generic output, the brands and products that win are the ones with a real point of view about a real person. A model can generate a thousand on-brief options. It can't tell you which one a tired parent at 9pm will actually trust. That judgment, rooted in the audience, is becoming the scarce part of the job. Starting there isn't a soft skill. It's the systems decision that makes everything downstream cheaper and sharper.
