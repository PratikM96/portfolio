A CS Degree Is a Creative Director's Secret Weapon

NYUSystems thinking · craftApril 9, 20265 min read

I studied computer science at NYU before I ran a single brand. For years it felt like a detour. It was the opposite. The most useful thing I bring to a creative engagement is not a design instinct, it is a habit of thinking in systems. And in 2026, with AI sitting in the middle of every workflow, that habit has gone from quietly useful to a genuine edge.

You stop designing screens and start designing systems

A CS education teaches you to see the structure underneath the surface. Not this button, but the state it belongs to. Not this layout, but the rule that generates every layout. When I designed three DealNews sub-brands that still read as one network, I was not making three logos. I was defining what stays constant and what is allowed to flex, the same way you would design a component library. Brand architecture is a systems problem wearing a visual costume.

You can actually talk to the people who build it

Design dies in the handoff. A creative who cannot speak to an engineer hands over a beautiful file and hopes. A creative who understands constraints, state, and edge cases hands over something buildable, and gets in the room when the trade-offs happen. That fluency is why the work I direct tends to survive contact with production instead of getting watered down in it.

Art school teaches you to make the thing. A systems foundation teaches you to design the machine that makes a thousand things, on-brand, without you in the loop.

Data stops being scary and starts being a tool

Comfort with data changes the conversation. At The Forest Road Company I built executive analytics frameworks tracking CAC, engagement, and ROI, because creative that cannot point to a number gets cut first. The CS background means I read the analytics myself instead of waiting for a report. I know which numbers are signal and which are noise, and I design against the ones that matter.

Why it is an advantage now, specifically

Generative AI rewards people who think in systems. The difference between a novelty user and someone who actually compresses a production cycle is whether you can design the pipeline: the inputs, the constraints, the points where a human has to step in and judge. That is engineering thinking applied to creative work. The tools will keep getting better at execution. The advantage moves to the people who can architect how the work gets made, not just make it.
