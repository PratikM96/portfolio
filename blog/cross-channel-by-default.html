Cross-Channel by Default: One Idea, Held Steady From the Brand Decision to the Fifteen-Second Cut

SPORTIME · esports conceptBrand · motion · UI/UX · growthApril 14, 20265 min read

A brand identity, an app, a launch film, and a performance campaign get scoped as four projects, handed to four teams, and stitched together in the last week before launch. The seams always show. The audience does not experience your work in four neat buckets, they experience one thing, in one feed, on one phone, in the gap between two other things they care about more. So the work has to be conceived as one thing, by someone who can hold all of it in their head at once. Every discipline, one thread.

The handoff is where the idea dies

Every handoff is a small game of telephone, and the message that comes out the far end is always blander than the one that went in. The brand team sets a tone in a deck. The product team reads the deck and interprets it onto a screen. The motion team gets a flat static comp and guesses at how any of it is supposed to move. The growth team writes the ads last, under the most deadline pressure, and flattens whatever is left to fit a 1080 by 1080 frame and a four-word headline. Nobody on that chain did bad work, and that is the cruel part. Everyone was competent and the result was still generic, because the idea lost a few percent of its resolution at every border it crossed, until what shipped was the average of four good-faith interpretations. And the cost is not only quality, it is time and money, because every border needs its own kickoff, its own review cycle, and a reconciliation pass at the end to glue the pieces back into something that looks intentional. That reconciliation is where budgets quietly go, paying four teams to re-find an agreement that one team would never have lost. The fix is not better handoff documents or a longer kickoff. It is fewer borders to cross in the first place.

One person holding the arc keeps it sharp

When one person holds the whole arc, the brand decision and the distribution decision can argue with each other in real time instead of in two meetings a month apart. On the SPORTIME program in 2023, the identity, the video-first content system, and the performance side were not sequenced one after another, they were designed as one thing, so a choice about how a logo behaved in motion was also a choice about how a Reel opened in the first half second, which is also a choice about cost per thousand. I was the sole marketing lead holding that throughline, with three to four direct reports and a creative lead at each club running to the same strategy, which is what let the decisions stay connected at the scale of more than fifteen accounts. That integration is part of how the program reached 11.7M combined impressions across the year. Siloed, those layers would each have been optimized alone and the whole would have gone slack.

Siloed creative optimizes each piece and loses the whole. Integrated creative accepts a slightly less perfect logo to get a campaign that actually compounds.

It is the only way to design for a product that is also a brand

The argument stops being abstract the moment the product and the brand are the same object, which is the work I am most drawn to now. In the esports membership concept I built, the identity, the app UI, the broadcast motion, and the social engine are not four adjacent deliverables that need aligning, they are one system, and a single decision in the brand layer changes a screen, a broadcast lower-third, and a social cut in the same stroke. Pick the wrong accent color and you have not just made an ugly logo, you have made an unreadable scoreboard and a Reel that dies in the feed. You cannot split that across four teams and four briefs and expect it to hold together, because the dependencies run through everything, the way they do in actual software. The throughline has to live in one head that can see the brand, the build, and the feed at the same time, or it does not survive the first real decision.

What this asks of one mind

This is a generalist's argument in an era that quietly rewards specialists, and it asks for fluency across four crafts, not mastery of one. The point was never to be mediocre at four things, it is to be fluent enough across brand, motion, UI/UX, and growth to keep one idea steady as it travels from a brand decision to a screen to a fifteen-second cut that has to land in a feed. What makes that possible is not raw talent at four crafts, it is the habit of seeing the system under the surface, the same habit a computer science background drills in. You learn to model a brand as a set of rules and relationships, to decide what stays constant and what is allowed to flex, so one decision updates a hundred screens instead of one. Holding a single idea across brand, build, and feed was always the hard part. Seeing the structure beneath all three is what lets one person hold it.

FAQ

When is splitting into specialist teams actually the right call?+
When the channels genuinely do not touch, or when one of them is deep enough to be its own discipline, a broadcast spot at film-craft level, a data-heavy app with real engineering risk. The argument is against splitting one idea by default, not against specialists. The test is whether a decision in one layer changes another. When it does, keep them together. When it truly does not, split with a clear conscience.
Does holding the arc mean one person does everything alone?+
No, and that would not scale past a single project. It means one person owns the intent and the connections between layers, then leads specialists who go deeper than any generalist could. At SPORTIME that was a creative lead at each club running to one strategy. The arc is held by one mind, the craft is delivered by a team.
What is the real cost of working this way?+
You trade a few points of local perfection for coherence. The logo might be slightly less precious than a brand specialist working in isolation would have made it, and the spot slightly less virtuosic. You accept that on purpose, because a campaign that holds together end to end beats four flawless pieces that do not add up. The honest version of this argument owns that tradeoff instead of pretending it is free.
