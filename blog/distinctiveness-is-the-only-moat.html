Distinctiveness Is the Only Moat Left

Brand systemsBrand strategy · positioningMay 4, 20266 min read

For a few years, a competent brand was enough to compete. Clean type, a sensible palette, an on-trend tone, and you looked credible. That era is ending. When every competitor has the same generative tools producing the same competent output, competent is the new invisible. The only durable advantage left is a point of view sharp enough that a model couldn't have generated it.

The average is now free, so the average is worthless

Generative tools are extremely good at the middle of the distribution. Ask for a fintech brand and you get a tasteful blue system that looks like every other fintech brand, instantly, for free. That's not a knock on the tools. It's the point. When the safe, on-trend version is a commodity anyone can summon, choosing it is choosing to disappear. Distinctiveness used to be a nice-to-have for brands with budget. It's becoming the entire game.

Distinctiveness is a strategy decision, not a style decision

This is where it gets misread. People hear distinctive and think louder, weirder, more color. That's style. Real distinctiveness comes from a position: a specific belief about a specific audience that you're willing to design against even when it's uncomfortable. In the prediction-market concept I built, the entire system flows from one stance, that the responsible, legible product can win in a category racing the other way. The look follows the position. It's not the other way around.

A point of view is the one input a model doesn't have. It's the last thing that has to come from a person who decided something.

It shows up in the numbers, not just the awards

This isn't an argument for art over outcomes. Distinctive, emotionally specific creative consistently out-earns generic work, and the gap is widening as generic gets cheaper. A brand that stands for something gets remembered, gets chosen, and gets a premium. A brand that looks like the category gets compared on price. Distinctiveness is the most commercial decision a brand can make right now, dressed up as a creative one.

What this asks of the work

It means the hard part of the job moves upstream, to the decision about what you actually believe and who you're really for. The execution is increasingly handled by the production layer. The differentiation lives in the strategy, the restraint, the willingness to be a clear something instead of a tasteful anything. In a market drowning in competent output, having a spine is the moat.
