Cross-Channel by Default: Why It Should Be One Engagement, Not Four

SPORTIME · Cloud9 conceptBrand · motion · UI/UX · growthApril 14, 20266 min read

A brand identity, an app, a launch film, and a performance campaign get scoped as four projects, handed to four teams, and stitched together at the end. The seams always show. The audience doesn't experience your work in four buckets. They experience one thing, in one feed, on one phone. So the work should be conceived as one thing too.

The handoff is where the idea dies

Every handoff is a small game of telephone. The brand team sets a tone, the product team interprets it, the motion team gets a static comp and guesses at the movement, the growth team writes the ads last and flattens it all to fit the channel. Nobody did bad work. The idea just lost a little resolution at each border until what shipped was generic. The fix isn't better handoff documents. It's fewer borders.

One person holding the arc keeps it sharp

When I work an engagement, brand, motion, UI/UX, and growth are the same conversation. At SPORTIME, the identity, the video-first content system, and the performance side weren't sequenced, they were designed together, which is part of how the program pushed impressions from roughly 4.5M to over 11.7M in a year. The brand decision and the distribution decision informed each other in real time instead of in separate meetings a month apart.

Siloed creative optimizes each piece and loses the whole. Integrated creative accepts a slightly less perfect logo to get a campaign that actually compounds.

It is the only way to design for a product that is also a brand

This matters most in the work I'm drawn to now: products that are also brands. In the Cloud9 membership concept, the identity, the app UI, the broadcast motion, and the social engine aren't adjacent deliverables, they're one system where a decision in the brand layer changes a screen and a social cut at the same time. You can't silo that and have it hold together. The throughline has to be held by someone who can see all of it at once.

What this asks of a creative director

This is a generalist's argument, and generalists are unfashionable. But the point isn't to be mediocre at four things. It's to be fluent enough across all of them to hold the idea steady as it moves from a brand decision to a screen to a fifteen-second cut. AI makes this more possible, not less: the production layer absorbs more of the execution, which frees a director to do the thing AI can't, hold a single coherent intent across every surface the audience will actually touch.

FAQ

Isn't "cross-channel" just a buzzword for doing more?+
No, it's the opposite of more. It's refusing to split one idea into four briefs that drift apart. The audience meets your work as one thing in one feed, so it should be conceived as one thing.
