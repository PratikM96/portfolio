00 / Situation

The in-house designer who also ran social and paid

Two years running the brand's large social accounts and paid advertising while serving as the lead in-house designer. The social side and the design side moved together, not handed off.

DealNews is a long-running deals and e-commerce brand with a large, established audience. I came in as the lead in-house designer and also ran the brand's social accounts and paid advertising, producing the full range of digital collateral the network needed: advertiser-facing web pages, landing pages, app UI updates, sub-brand identities, infographics, newsletters, and new-business proposals.

advertise.dealnews.com, the advertiser-facing pages I designed for the B2B side.
01 / Marketplace · UX

Designing the advertiser-facing side

I designed the advertiser-facing pages for the B2B side in Adobe XD, from the homepage through the application flow, on a custom Shopify theme, and ran the B2B social channels alongside them. The aim was to make the advertiser side legible and self-explanatory for the small and mid-sized buyers the brand wanted to reach.

Products page, part of the advertiser-facing design.

02 / App UI · UX

Every tap was leaking trust

The app had drifted from the website and from the brand. I designed updates against the complaints showing up in store reviews: third-party sign-in, a real filter bar, brand-matched iconography, synced saved searches, and modal moments for tips and messages, carried through to dev handoff. Nothing was decoration. Every change pointed at a friction someone had already written a review about.

Every change pointed at a complaint.

Left: login with third-party sign-in, the new filter bar, and synced saved items. Right: the newsletter redesign across desktop and mobile.
03 / Sub-brand system · Brand

Three sub-brands that do not fight the parent

Three affiliate sites, Generator Tools, Refrigerator HQ, and Wake Up Sleepy Head, each had to stand on its own without snapping the thread back to DealNews. I built three logos with personality pulled from each product niche, held together by shared type and one structural logic, delivered across web, app icon, and avatar with guidelines for each. Distinct enough to live alone, related enough to read as family.

Three sub-brands, one network. Distinct personalities, shared structural logic.
04 / Graphics & marketing

The output nobody frames, and everybody needs

Under the three builds ran the daily work: sweepstakes graphics, seasonal campaigns, consumer survey infographics, newsletter redesigns for desktop and mobile, sales decks. The unglamorous proof that one system holds its consistency across a network of sites while shipping at volume.

Advertiser one-pagers and consumer survey infographics, part of an always-on output.
05 / Through-line

Social and design, one practice

Social and paid running alongside the design of the web pages, the app UI, the sub-brands, and the daily collateral. The social side and the design side as one connected practice, not handed off.

It worked best when the social side and the design side ran as one system instead of separate briefs. Running the creative and the channels together, rather than handing either off, is the operating model I have carried since.
