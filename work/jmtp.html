00 / Situation

Read the room before the room arrives

Two fundraisers, two completely different crowds, zero second takes in either. The work had to read each room before the room showed up. The record $650K day says it did.

The Johnny Mac Tennis Project opens tennis and real opportunity to students from under-resourced communities in East Harlem and the South Bronx. Its two annual fundraisers carry the budget: the Pro Am in the Hamptons and the Comedy Night in New York City, both stacked with tennis legends, donors, and press. The 2023 Pro Am put John and Patrick McEnroe, Jim Courier, James Blake, Mats Wilander, and Christina McHale on court among 128 players. The materials had to match that altitude, and there is no reshoot at a live event.

The Pro Am banner, built around the Hamptons setting.
01 / The work

Two rooms, two briefs, no overlap

Same cause, opposite visual problems. The Pro Am wanted the Hamptons: illustrated event banners built around the venue and the occasion, polished without going generic. The Comedy Night wanted energy and timing: I shot it live in a New York theater, then cut and delivered media-ready packages for social, editorial, and donor use. The discipline was refusing to impose one look on both.

Every asset delivered on schedule. No missed deadlines, no format gaps.

Pro Am on court, and Comedy Night from the live theater shoot.
02 / Pro Am campaign

A full sequence, not a flyer

Beyond the banner, the Pro Am ran a complete promotional sequence: ticket and feed graphics, an About and Sponsors carousel, a countdown series, and the event-day post. One campaign across ticket, feed, and story, with sponsor recognition built in.

One campaign across ticket, feed, and story, plus sponsor recognition and the countdown series.
03 / Comedy Night

Built from the live shoot

The Comedy Night carried its own social set across square posts and stories, all of it cut from the live theater photography, fast enough to run while the night was still news.

The Comedy Night set, feed and story from the live shoot.

Two rooms, two crowds, no second takes. The Pro Am wanted polish and the Hamptons, the Comedy Night wanted energy and timing, and shooting both back to back taught me to read an occasion before I impose a look on it. Live work does not reward your favorite style. It rewards the one the moment actually needs, and learning to put taste second to the room is a discipline I carry into every brief I art-direct now.
