The situation

No design team of its own

The US division of a global humanitarian nonprofit had no design team of its own. Every creative request funneled through the broader marketing function, a bottleneck for an organization running an expanding set of programs across food relief, healthcare, and education. The fix was never going to be more output from one person. It was a team.

01 / The work

A team built to outlast me

I built and led the volunteer marketing team, growing it from 5 to over 15 members across four years. As design demand grew beyond core marketing, I spun off a dedicated 5-person design team and set the workflows, the briefing process, and the quality standards that let it handle every collateral request on its own, mentoring the junior designers as it scaled. From day one the goal was a team that held its own standard without me there to enforce it. Across those years it produced brand animation, social graphics, brochures, posters, newsletters, and recap videos.

The deliverable

A team built to run without me, holding one standard across every request.

02 / Campaigns & social

The feed at team scale

Around it sat seasonal and event social, milestone posts, and recaps, all held to one visual system across the team's run.

What it taught me

By 2018 design demand had grown enough that the design team split formally out of marketing into its own function, built to sustain output long after I stepped back. The brief was never really the brochures. It was a creative function that could outlast me, and growing the marketing team from five volunteers to fifteen-plus, then spinning off a dedicated design team, meant designing workflows, standards, and people. Scaling a team and handing off my own judgment is the part that reads as leadership rather than craft, and it is what I lean on hardest when I make the case for running a department.
