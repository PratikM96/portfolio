Distinctiveness Is the Only Moat Left: When the Average Is Free, the Average Is Worthless

Brand systemsBrand strategy · positioningMay 4, 20265 min read

For a few years, a competent brand was enough to compete. Clean type, a sensible palette, an on-trend tone of voice, and you looked credible enough to stay in the game. That era is closing. When every competitor runs the same generative tools producing the same tasteful, on-trend output, competent stops being a baseline and becomes the new invisible. The only durable advantage left is a point of view sharp enough that a model could not have generated it, because everything a model can generate is, by definition, now available to everyone else too.

The average is now free, so the average is worthless

Generative tools are extraordinarily good at the exact middle of the distribution, which is the one place a brand cannot afford to live anymore. Ask a model for a fintech identity and you get a tasteful blue system, a clean sans-serif, a calm gradient, and a tone that reassures without saying anything, instantly and for nothing, indistinguishable from every other fintech brand that asked the same model the same week. That is not a complaint about the tools, it is the entire point of them, they are designed to return the safe consensus answer. When the safe, on-trend version is a commodity anyone can summon in a minute, choosing it is choosing to disappear into a crowd of identical choices. Distinctiveness used to be a nice-to-have, the thing brands with budget and confidence reached for after the basics were handled. It is becoming the whole game, because the basics are now free and free things do not differentiate anyone.

Distinctiveness is a strategy decision, not a style decision

Distinctiveness is a strategy decision long before it is a style decision, and confusing the two is where most brands go wrong. People hear distinctive and reach for louder, weirder, more color, a brighter palette and a noisier layout, as if difference were a volume knob. That is style, and style copied without a reason underneath reads as costume. Real distinctiveness comes from a position, a specific belief about a specific audience that you are willing to design against even when it costs you and even when it is uncomfortable in the room. In the prediction-market concept I built, the entire system flows from a single stance, that a responsible, genuinely legible product can win in a category that is racing hard in the other direction toward hype and opacity. Every real decision, the restraint in the interface, the plain language, the refusal to gamify, follows from that belief. The look is downstream of the position, never the reverse, and a brand that picks a look before it picks a belief is just decorating an empty room.

A point of view is the one input a model doesn't have. It's the last thing that has to come from a person who decided something.

It shows up in the numbers, not just the awards

This is not an argument for art over outcomes, it is an argument that distinctiveness is now the more commercial of the two choices. Distinctive, emotionally specific creative consistently out-earns generic work, and the gap between them is widening precisely because generic keeps getting cheaper to produce, which floods the market with more of it and makes the rare distinctive thing stand out harder. The mechanism is not mysterious. A brand that stands for something clear gets remembered, gets chosen on purpose, and earns the room to charge a premium, while a brand that looks like its whole category has handed the buyer no reason to choose it on anything but price, and price is a race you win only by losing margin. I have watched a category-leading look lose to a sharper, stranger, more specific one on a real decision, not because it was prettier but because it gave a person something to believe and a reason to pick it. Distinctiveness is the most commercial decision a brand can make right now, quietly dressed up as a creative one.

What this asks of the work

Distinctiveness moves the hard part of the job upstream, away from execution and onto the decision about what you actually believe and who you are really for. That is the uncomfortable relocation a lot of creative work has not caught up to yet. The execution, the rendering and the variants and the polish, is increasingly cheap and increasingly automated, which means craft alone no longer separates anyone, because everyone has the same craft on tap. The differentiation that survives lives further up, in the strategy, the restraint, the willingness to stand for something specific instead of something tasteful and forgettable. It takes more nerve to commit to a position than to approve a safe one, because a position can be wrong and a safe choice can only be invisible, and invisible feels safer right up until it is fatal. In a market drowning in competent output, a point of view is the moat, and it is the one part of the work that still has to come from a person who decided something and was willing to be held to it.

FAQ

How does a brand actually find its position, not just claim one?+
Look for the thing you are willing to lose customers over. A real position has an opposite that someone reasonable would prefer, which means it excludes people on purpose. If your stated belief is something no competitor would ever disagree with, like we put customers first, it is not a position, it is wallpaper. The test is whether saying it out loud costs you anyone. If it costs nobody, it differentiates nobody.
What do you tell a client who finds a sharp position too risky?+
Reframe the risk, because the safe option is the actually dangerous one now. Blending in does not lower risk in a flooded market, it guarantees the slow death of being compared on price forever. The conservative move and the distinctive move have swapped places, and the genuinely cautious choice today is to stand for something specific before the category fills entirely with competent sameness.
Can a brand be too distinctive?+
Yes, when distinctiveness floats free of the audience and becomes difference for its own sake. A position has to be true to a real person's need, not just unlike the competition. The failure mode is a brand so busy being unlike everyone that it stops being legible or useful to anyone. Sharp and clear is the target. Strange and self-indulgent is a different, quieter way to disappear.
