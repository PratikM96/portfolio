The situation

A network posting into the void

A network this size does not have a content problem. It has a systems problem wearing a content problem's clothes. SPORTIME runs the largest tennis club network in the Northeast, three verticals, tennis clubs, a new pickleball division, and the John McEnroe Tennis Academy, each needing its own identity while staying coherent inside the parent.

The social presence was stagnant. Accounts across 15+ locations were barely growing and posting inconsistently, far below what a network that size should pull. At the same time, the marketing was built around a $75M JMTA facility expansion that had to be ready before opening day.

01 / Brand system

I stopped designing posts and built the thing that makes them

One engine to run all of it. Five brand marks in a single system, SPORTIME Clubs, JMTA, Pickleball, Volleyball, and individual club identities, delivered in full source files across every format and colorway with a usage guide so nothing drifted. The decision that mattered was the unglamorous one: build the system first, slower up front, instead of making a better post this week.

SPORTIME brand header motion, the system in movement.
02 / SPORTIME Tennis

Video-first, run by the clubs

We rebuilt the content model across all 13 club locations. A video-first approach replaced the static-heavy feeds, because video is where attention actually is, a shared calendar coordinated output across every location, and a templated kit plus event photography let the staff at each club ship on-brand without a designer in the room.

Winter camp set, one template carrying a season of variants the club teams ran themselves.
Result

From a near-zero posting baseline to 7,354 Instagram posts by early 2024, 3,115 of them Reels. Instagram posts up 510 percent year over year in 2023, Reels up 2,115 percent. Facebook organic impressions across the network up 83 percent to 2.4M.

HEAD Speed equipment-partner drop cut for feed and story, and the Road to NYC season campaign in print.

03 / JMTA

A $75M campus, and one shot at opening day

JMTA was expanding across two campuses, a $75M build the marketing had to be ready for before a single court opened. There is no soft launch for a facility that size. Randall's Island would open as the largest indoor tennis training facility in the world, and the enrollment had to move from day one.

I ran the whole program into that deadline: event graphics, Team Cup materials, seasonal campaigns, and co-branded SPORTIME and JMTA collateral, delivered at once across print vendors, social, and internal comms. One system, every surface, on time.

The Combine, one campaign across flyer, feed, and story. This is what the system was built to do.
Result

Every asset shipped before opening. Within the year, JMTA became the highest-reach account in the network, its largest at 15,154 followers (+3,131 for the year), and the top page on both platforms by organic impressions. The new facility reached about 65% court utilization within 90 days of opening.

Scholarship and tryouts collateral from the same kit, and the JMTA brand header motion.

04 / SPORTIME Pickleball

The fastest-growing sport in America, built to scale without me

Pickleball was the fastest-growing sport in the country, and SPORTIME's new division needed a brand that could stand on its own across 200+ courts without a designer at every tournament. The trap with a hot category is making noise. I made a system instead: a cleaner logo and badge, then a template library for tournament flyers, gift cards, open-play promos, and event series, with motion to carry the launch.

Pickleball launch trailer, cut to carry the vertical rollout.
Result

Launched across 200+ Northeast courts with a full brand and content system in place. Pickleball-specific inquiries up about 20% in the six months after the rebrand. On Facebook, pickleball ran the network's highest engagement rate at 16.7%.

Template-library flyers and the feed system at work, the dedicated courts, and the clinic community that filled them.

What it taught me

The lesson was not that better content wins. It was that members never showed up for a polished one-off. They showed up for content that was consistent, fast, and recognizably theirs, and the only thing that produces that at the scale of 15+ accounts is an engine, not effort. I went in as the person who could make the work and left as the person who built the system that makes it, and ran the team that keeps it running after the brief is closed. That shift, from maker to the operator who owns the channel end to end, is the job I want to do at a larger scale.
