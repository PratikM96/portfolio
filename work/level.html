A booming category, racing into a harm problem

Prediction markets exploded in 2026, and the field is sprinting toward a trillion-dollar future while a documented youth-gambling problem builds underneath it. Sportsbooks are flooding in, optimizing for engagement, and the people most at risk are the easiest to reach. Level is the responsible one, built to still be the one people actually want: plain-language odds, an explainer in front of every market, and protection on by default, in the corner of the category where it matters most. Industry figures are sourced in the research; none describe this project.

Lead with responsibility, in the corner where it is hardest

The easy read was wrong. The premise that every rival looks like crypto-gambling no longer holds: the market leader is already clean. So Level does not win on calmer visuals. It wins on a designed system of protection and legibility, and it plants that flag in sports, the most lucrative and most harmful corner, where the rest of the field is gamifying hardest. Responsible by default: limits on by default, an honest Starter mode for the 18 to 20 cohort, no engagement bait. Legible by default: plain-language odds and an assistant that explains a market before you stake. Built to grow up: sports now, the wider world later, category-agnostic so it extends into economics and politics without a relaunch.

Protection, designed as a system

The responsible-use layer is not a disclaimer, it is the product, designed first and built into every screen, and it is the senior-judgment proof. Friction that protects: limits on by default, raising one takes a day and lowering is instant, cooldowns after a losing run, and an understand-before-you-stake gate on unfamiliar markets. The inverse of an engagement model: the same behavioral model the category uses to maximize time-on-app, pointed the other way, watching for problem patterns and intervening with a check-in and resources, never an upsell. Honest by construction: fees and the house position shown up front, the real net on the stake screen, no streaks, no urgency theater. Self-exclusion is one tap, with no friction to leave.

The flow under protection: find a market, stake with friction that protects, and stay in control, with the limit traveling the whole way.

Understand it first. Then stake.

The assistant sits in front of every market. Ask it anything in plain language, and it is also the gate: the stake button stays locked until you have the basics, what the number means and what it resolves on. In a category that hides the odds behind a slick interface, Level puts a teacher in front of the stake. You cannot stake on a market you have not understood, which is the whole concept in one interaction. Motion does the legibility work and carries the brand's feel, easing to rest, never flashing, the index moving last, and reduced-motion aware.

Where this goes

Level launches into a category projected to grow several times over by 2030, carrying a trust deficit no incumbent is addressing. The sports share of volume is forecast to fall from over 60% to about 30% by 2030, which is why Level is built to extend past sports. Category figures are sourced; Level's own targets are projections to validate after launch, not results. What it is positioned to capture: the responsible segment that wants in but distrusts the field, a high rate of users who clear the explainer gate before their first stake, and a trust halo with parents, regulators, and press that compounds as scrutiny on the category grows.

The brand system at a glance, built around the position, not onto it.
What it demonstrates

Level started as an argument with the easy version of the brief. The obvious move was calmer visuals, but the category's real problem is not how gambling looks, it is what it does, so I built the concept around protection and legibility as product features. Designing restraint on purpose taught me that a point of view is worth more than a nicer interface. Making speculative work like this is how I show the strategic judgment a creative director is hired for, not just the craft.
