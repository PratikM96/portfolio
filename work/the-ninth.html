00 / The concept

Merch is not membership

Esports is shifting from one-off merch toward subscription ecosystems, the move the industry calls Membership 2.0, and the orgs that fold access and digital status into real tiers retain better. One of the most valuable brands in North American esports, built on a calm, premium, fashion-credible register that is the deliberate opposite of aggressive gamer branding, has never built a year-round home for its fandom. The Ninth answers that as an additive membership sub-brand, named for the three nines in the Cloud9 mark and for cloud nine itself, running on one idea, altitude, and one rule, the summit is earned, never sold. The top tier, Inner Nine, cannot be bought anywhere in the product.

01 / The sub-brand

A system built on altitude

A full system for the org, built from the IX monogram, an altitude palette anchored on the brand's signature blue, type, voice, and motion. The member card is the hero object. Color runs as an altitude gradient with no warm accents, so the premium signal at the summit is frost, not gold. The voice stays calm at altitude, confidence without volume, no caps-lock, no countdown urgency. If a line reads like a hype drop or a casino, it is wrong.

02 / The app

Built to earn a dead Tuesday

Mobile-first, because 56% of esports viewing is on mobile. Every surface feeds one loop, watch, predict, belong, climb. Predict is free-to-play in points, never money, and those points feed the climb, which keeps the engine on the right side of the calm brand. Community lives inside the app, so tier roles create status where the org controls the surface, with the member card as the live status object at the center.

03 / The clipper

A working AI moment-clipper, not a mockup

Of four AI features, the clipper is the one I prototyped for real, and the one the product leads with. It serves the casual fans who do not know when to look up, and it is credible now, because real clippers already detect plays off kill feeds, objective captures, and audio or chat spikes. Three live signals fuse, the system fires only when they agree, and an auto-clip rises to the fan the instant the moment happens. The AI handles detection and a first-draft caption, and the team still reviews before anything ships.

04 / Broadcast

The membership, on the broadcast

The overlay package carries the membership onto the broadcast, not just the brand. Alongside the score bug and lower-third, it puts members co-watching on screen, ties the broadcast moment to the same clipper with the same frost IX, and celebrates a member reaching Inner Nine live on air. The motion rises and eases, never bounces, and frost is rationed to the two summit moments.

05 / Social

One match in, a week out

One match produces a week of cross-platform output across a managed set of accounts, because that fan-out reaches far more fans than a single official account at the same cost. The clipper is the top of the funnel: each broadcast day yields 10 to 25 moments, the AI drafts platform-native variants per template, a human approves and tunes voice, and the set fans out on an event-driven cadence. Nothing publishes without a human gate, which is how the calm-premium voice survives at that volume.

What it taught me

One problem solved across brand, product, AI, motion, and social at once, with the decisions visible and defended. The recurring move is strategic restraint, free-to-play not gambling, earned not sold, calm not loud, owned community not outsourced, AI for volume but taste kept human. That restraint is the senior call in every layer, and it is the throughline this kind of work has to hold. The Ninth was me designing for belonging instead of buying, and prototyping the AI moment-clipper for real instead of faking it in a mockup.
