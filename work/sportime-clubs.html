The call

Not a content problem

SPORTIME runs the largest tennis club network in the Northeast, three sub-brands across 13 locations. The feeds did not match the size of the business, and more posting would only have spread the same inconsistency across 15 accounts. So I made the unglamorous call: build the system first, slower up front, instead of shipping a better post that week. The marketing also had to carry the JMTA flagship expansion, a $75M project that had to be ready before opening day.

01 / The architecture

Five marks, one system

SPORTIME Clubs, JMTA, Pickleball, Volleyball, and the individual clubs, in one architecture. Each mark stands on its own and still reads as family, delivered in full source files across every format and colorway with a usage guide so nothing drifts.

One system, five marks, built to hold together from a phone feed to a dome banner.
02 / The engine, demonstrated

One template becomes a season

The whole point of the system is that one template becomes a campaign becomes a season. Here is a single event, the JMTA Combine, going from flyer to feed to story, the kit the club staff ran themselves without a designer in the room.

One campaign across three surfaces, built so the staff could ship the next one in minutes, on-brand.

03 / Video-first

Built for the feed

Video replaced the static-heavy feeds, because that is where the attention is. Brand headers, launch films, and partner drops, cut for the feed and handed to the clubs as a repeatable format.

Result

From a near-zero baseline to 7,354 Instagram posts by early 2024, 3,115 of them Reels. Instagram posting up 510 percent year over year in 2023, Reels up 2,115 percent, and Facebook organic impressions across the network up 83 percent to 2.4M.

04 / Run by the clubs

Thirteen locations, one brand

The system only works if the people at each club can run it without it drifting. I shot the network too, from Randall's Island to Bethpage, Roslyn, and Schenectady, the physical proof the feed was selling.

Shot across the network, the rooms the feed was promising.

05 / The same engine, very different brands

One system, stretched

The same kit held three sub-brands that could not be more different: a $75M flagship, the country's fastest-growing sport, and a scrappy channel shot on a camera.

JMTA, the flagship

Every asset shipped before the new Randall's Island campus opened. The $75M build has since added a $55M expansion, completed April 2025, to become the largest indoor tennis training facility in the US. JMTA became the network's largest account at 15,154 followers.

Pickleball, built to scale

A brand that could stand on its own without a designer at every tournament. The vertical I launched is heading toward 200-plus courts across 15-plus tri-state clubs by 2031, with new locations opening through 2025 and into 2026.

Volleyball, shot fast

No collateral, so I built the channel on a camera, shot and posted fast enough to ride the weekend it happened on. It became one of the network's strongest accounts by organic engagement, on a smaller following.

2023 alone: 4.4M organic Instagram impressions, up 62 percent, with 117K engagements, and 6.1M Facebook impressions, up 105 percent, with 207K engagements, on more than 3,000 videos published across the network.

What it taught me

The lesson was not that better content wins. Members never showed up for a polished one-off. They showed up for content that was consistent, fast, and recognizably theirs, and the only thing that produces that across 15 accounts is an engine, not effort. I came in able to make the work and left having built the system that makes it and the team that keeps it running after the brief closes. That shift, from maker to operator owning the channel end to end, is the work I want to do at a larger scale.
