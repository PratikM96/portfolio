Case study · Designing the system

The Brand
System

How this portfolio was designed and built. Not a static style sheet, a living system: color, type, layout, motion, and voice, each shown in the same code that runs the site.

(00)

The Brief

One person, two audiences. Hiring managers and brand leadership scanning fast for range and seniority, and design-literate viewers judging taste and craft. The system had to land a strong first impression and hold up on the second and third look.

The brand sits where most people keep three things apart: design taste, engineering systems-thinking, and marketing accountability. A CS degree from NYU is the reason the work thinks in systems first and ties to measurable outcomes. The site is built to prove that, not just claim it.

01

Systems-minded

Structured and built to scale. Nothing one-off that should have been a system. One stylesheet drives every page.

02

Cross-channel

Brand, motion, UI/UX, social, and performance read as one connected practice, never separate tracks.

03

Audience-first

Leads with what the viewer knows and responds to. Organizational detail layers in after.

04

Accountable

Outcomes attached. Real numbers, never inflated, never vague.

05

Quietly confident

Lets the work carry the weight. No overclaiming, no jargon, no filler.

The two-accent rule

The accents never cross. Teal carries identity and interaction; coral marks the annotation layer. This keeps both meaningful instead of decorative, and it is the rule that makes the palette feel designed rather than busy.

Teal · identity & interaction

  • Links, hovers, and focus states
  • Filters and active nav
  • The marquee
  • Heading accent words
  • Result-box outline

Coral · annotation layer

  • Project years
  • Section index numbers
  • Experience & chapter numbers
  • Result labels
  • Availability dot, footer CTA hover

No pure white or pure black anywhere. Accents never fill large areas or set body copy, the one exception being the marquee, which is deliberately loud. All text holds WCAG AA contrast.

(03)

Wordmark

No illustrated logo, and the brand does not need one. The wordmark is typographic: the name in the display face, heavy and uppercase, with a small © as a signature mark.

Layout & Imagery

A 12-column grid, generous vertical rhythm, hairline dividers, and asymmetry that lets type break the grid. Whitespace is structural, never filler.

Grid12-column, ~1380px max width, 4vw gutters

DividersHairline at 14% tint above section heads, never solid rules
Corner radius4 to 6px. Never pill-rounded

(06)

Voice

The writing is as much a part of the brand as the visuals: concise, technically fluent, outcome-led, no preamble. Lead with the problem and the result, then show how it was made.

Do

  • Lead with the outcome, then the process
  • Use specific, verified numbers
  • Short, declarative sentences
  • Treat method as a production layer, not a novelty
  • Match tone to the audience and seniority

Avoid

  • Em-dashes, in any format, absolute
  • Fabricated metrics, titles, or credentials
  • Generic filler phrasing: unlock, elevate, seamless, passionate
  • Vague superlatives with no number behind them
  • Over-formal résumé-speak and stacked adjectives
