The situation

No tolerance for ambiguity

Pipeline Medical needed a platform where healthcare procurement teams could browse, evaluate, and purchase medical supplies and capital equipment online. No brand, no digital presence, no existing channel to reach buyers. The site needed to communicate credibility and clarity for an audience with no tolerance for ambiguity in a purchasing decision.

01 / Brand identity

A clinical, credible mark

Developed a logo system built for digital and print applications: primary horizontal lockup, vertical variant, and full-color RGB versions. Clean, professional, and clinical, the right register for a B2B medical supplier serving procurement teams.

The rebrand: where the mark started, and the system it became.
02 / Marketplace UX

A procurement workflow, not a website

Designed the full website across three primary screens: Landing, Products catalog, and Shopping Cart. The UX was built around a procurement workflow: clear product categories, a browsable catalog, and a cart flow designed for bulk or repeat purchasing. Supported by Google display ads targeting medical equipment and injectable supply audiences, social assets, and informational collateral.

Catalog organized around procurement categories, and a cart flow built for bulk and repeat purchasing.
03 / Ads, collateral & social

The launch toolkit

Google display ads by product category, buyer-education one-pagers, the Capital Equipment List the procurement team worked from, and a brand social presence rounded out the launch toolkit.

Display ads by product category, buyer-education one-pagers, the Capital Equipment List, and the social header that anchored launch.
The result

Built to outlast the brief

The platform launched at supplies.pipelinemedical.com and remains live today, having expanded into pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, and GLP-1 medications, with distribution partnerships including Allergan, 3M, Molnlycke, and Provista. Its continued growth well beyond the original scope is evidence of the foundation that the first build established.

What it taught me

Pipeline was my early proof that good structure outlives its brief. Procurement buyers have zero tolerance for ambiguity, so every screen had to earn trust before it asked for a decision, and building it that way is why the platform is still live and scaling well past what I designed. Designing for the foundation instead of the launch is a long-game instinct that separates a brand lead from a project designer. It's the kind of durable thinking I want to be hired for.
