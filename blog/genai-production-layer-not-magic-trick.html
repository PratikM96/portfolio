GenAI Is a Production Layer, Not a Magic Trick

MethodologyWorkflow · brief to deliveryApril 19, 20266 min read

There are two ways to talk about generative AI in creative work, and both are wrong. One says it changes everything and a prompt replaces a team. The other says it's a toy that makes slop. The honest version is duller and more useful: GenAI is a production layer. It sits inside an existing process, it cuts turnaround and extends range, and it's only as good as the judgment steering it.

Where it actually earns its place

I use it at the points where speed compounds. Moodboarding and visual direction, so a concept can be seen before it's committed to. Motion previs, to test whether an idea reads in movement before the hours go into After Effects. Variant generation, to take one piece of work and produce the platform-native cuts a campaign needs. Copy drafting, to get a structured first pass I can then make sound like a person. None of these are the creative act. They're the friction around it, and that's exactly what a production layer should absorb.

Where it quietly hurts

The danger isn't bad output, it's plausible output. AI is very good at generating something that looks finished and is subtly off-brief, off-brand, or just generic. If you accept that because it's fast, you ship the average of the internet with your logo on it. The discipline is to treat every generation as a draft that has to clear the same bar as anything else, and to throw away the ones that are merely competent. Faster only helps if the standard doesn't move.

The goal is faster output without the shortcuts showing. The second that seam is visible, the speed wasn't worth it.

Specialized tools beat the all-in-one promise

The pitch is always one platform that does everything. In practice the value lives in specialized tools used for the one thing each is genuinely good at. Midjourney for art direction, Runway for motion exploration, a language model for copy systems and structure. Stitching the right specialists into a workflow beats a single generalist tool that does ten things at a six out of ten. This is a systems decision, not a software-shopping decision.

The human stays where the judgment is

Across every speculative brand system I've built recently, the pattern is the same: AI accelerates the pipeline, a person owns the taste. The model gives me ten directions in the time it used to take to make one. Choosing which one is right for this audience, on this problem, is the part that doesn't get automated, and it's the part clients are actually paying for. Used this way, GenAI doesn't make the creative director less necessary. It makes the role more about direction and less about labor, which is where it should have been all along.

FAQ

Will a prompt replace a creative team?+
No. GenAI is a production layer, not a replacement. It absorbs the friction around the creative act, moodboarding, previs, variants, drafts, but the judgment steering it is still the job, and it's the part clients pay for.
