AI Product Design

The Form Should Be the Disclosure

WISP conceptAI persona · brand · ethicsJune 4, 20266 min read

Every AI persona on the market is solving the wrong problem. The human-passing ones spend their entire design budget hiding that they are synthetic, which lands them in the uncanny valley and, increasingly, on the wrong side of disclosure law. When I designed a speculative AI host, I took the opposite position. Make the entity honestly, structurally synthetic, so the form itself does the disclosing.

Stop faking a face you do not have

The concept, WISP, is a live host with no human or character form. No face, no body. A presence made of light, motion, and voice. That one choice resolves two problems at once. The uncanny valley disappears, because there is no face to fall short of being real. And the honesty problem disappears, because the entity never claims to be a person, so it has nothing to hide. You free up the whole design budget for something nobody else is building: a non-human presence that is still emotionally legible.

Honesty as a design system, not a label

Disclosure is usually treated as a checkbox, a small "AI-generated" tag in the corner that the experience otherwise contradicts. That is backwards. If the experience is built to feel human and a label says it is not, the label loses. Designing honesty into the form means the truth is carried by the thing itself, in how it looks, moves, and speaks, not in fine print fighting against the rest of the design. The most trustworthy disclosure is one the user cannot miss because it is the entity.

A human-passing persona has to keep a secret. A presence made of light has nothing to hide, so it can be genuinely good company.

Compliance-native beats compliance-exposed

This is also the pragmatic call. Synthetic-performer disclosure laws are arriving, regulators are enforcing AI-content disclosure, and deepfake distrust is rising. An entity that is visibly, structurally synthetic is compliant by construction. A human-passing persona is the opposite: it inherits a growing legal and trust liability that compounds as the rules tighten. Designing for honesty up front is not just the ethical choice, it is the one that ages well.

The audience does not need it to cosplay as human

Underneath all of it is an audience-first bet. People raised on AI do not need their AI to pretend to be a person. They need it to be honest, expressive, and good to be around. The brands clinging to photoreal, human-passing personas are answering a question the audience stopped asking. The opportunity is to design AI that is unmistakably itself and still has presence and warmth. When the form is the disclosure, trust is not a feature you add. It is the starting condition.
