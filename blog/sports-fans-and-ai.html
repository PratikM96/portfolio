Sports, Fans, and AI: The Membership Products the Next Decade Needs

The Ninth concept · SPORTIMESports · membership · AIMay 28, 20265 min read

I've built brands for real sports audiences and a full speculative membership system for an esports org, and across both, one shift keeps surfacing. Fandom is moving away from one-off merch and tickets toward year-round membership, and AI is about to change how fans watch and how they belong. The orgs that win the next decade will design that shift on purpose, starting from the fan, instead of bolting an AI feature onto a store and calling it the future. The opening is real, and most of the category is looking at the wrong half of it.

The fan wants to belong on a Tuesday, not just on game day.

Start with the person on the other end, because the product follows from who they are. A real fan does not want another jersey, they want status inside the in-group, access to the culture, and a reason to show up when there is no match on the schedule. The category has historically sold them a shirt and a ticket and then gone quiet until the next home game, which leaves most of the year, and most of the relationship, on the table. Think about how the average fan actually spends their week, arguing about the lineup, watching clips, reading takes, checking in on the rival, almost none of which the official product participates in at all. The opening is a product that gives the fandom a year-round home with belonging and ritual at the center, where the checkout is a feature and not the entire point of the building, and the off-season is a season too.

AI is the layer that makes every match feel personal.

This is where the next decade gets genuinely interesting, and where the technology finally earns its place. A real-time AI layer can clip the moment something happens, tuned to your team and nobody else's, and surface the one play a casual fan needs to see so they never miss the thing everyone will be talking about tomorrow. It can act as a second commentator that already knows who you root for. Done well, that turns passive watching into something personal and participatory, which is exactly the daily-return loop a membership product lives or dies on. The broadcast has always been a one-to-many compromise, the same feed and the same commentary for millions of people who care about completely different things, and an AI layer is the first tool that can quietly break that compromise per fan. The point is not the AI, it is that the season starts to feel made for one person instead of broadcast at everyone, and that feeling is what a fan will actually pay to keep.

Membership 2.0 is not perks bolted onto a store. It is a home for the fandom, with a real-time AI layer that makes the whole season feel made for you.

The hard part is tone, not technology.

The technology is the easy half, and almost everyone underestimates how much that matters. Every org has a register, and the product has to feel like that org, not a generic gamer dashboard and not a casino with a logo dropped on top. In the membership concept I built, the constraint that drove every single decision was tone, calm, premium, and approachable, because that is who the org actually is, and an AI feature that ignored it would feel like a different company had wandered into the app overnight. This is the trap I watch teams fall into, they ship the same AI highlight reel and the same chat assistant every competitor has, and wonder why none of it builds loyalty, when the reason is that it feels like nobody specifically. The tech is mostly available to everyone now, which is precisely why it will not differentiate anyone. The taste call on voice is the moat, and it is the part you cannot buy off a shelf, because it is the part that has to come from actually knowing the fandom.

This pattern runs well past one league.

The same shape extends to a long list of fandoms nobody is serving well yet, which is what makes it worth writing down. Plenty of fragmented, under-served communities are content-system and membership opportunities sitting unbuilt, waiting for someone to design them from the fan inward instead of the merch outward. The biggest leagues will move slowest here, weighed down by legacy systems and a merch business they are reluctant to reframe, which leaves a real opening for smaller orgs and rising sports to design membership-first and skip the store-first era entirely. The through-line holds wherever you point it: design for what the fan actually wants, use AI to make the experience personal and the content scalable, and keep a human holding the brand's voice so the whole thing still sounds like somewhere you want to belong. The next great sports products will be membership systems with an AI heart and a very specific soul, and the soul is the harder half to get right, which is exactly why most of them will not.

FAQ

Which sports are best positioned for this membership shift first?+
The ones with passionate, digitally native, under-served fandoms, which is why esports and the smaller leagues are ahead of the legacy giants here. Big incumbents have the audience but heavy legacy systems and a merch business they are reluctant to reframe. A newer org can design membership-first from day one, with no store to protect, which is a real structural advantage.
How do you keep an AI personalization layer from feeling creepy to a fan?+
Keep it opt-in, legible, and pointed at delight rather than extraction. A fan is happy for the product to know their team and clip their highlights, and uneasy the second it feels like surveillance aimed at selling them something. The line is whether the personalization obviously serves the fan in the moment, so the design rule is simple: if a feature only benefits the org, it does not ship as personalization.
Does a smaller club without a big budget have any shot at building this?+
Yes, more than before, because the AI production layer that makes this work has gotten dramatically cheaper. A smaller org cannot outspend a league on rights, but it can out-design one on belonging, which is mostly a clarity-of-voice problem, not a budget problem. The expensive part used to be producing enough personalized content to sustain the loop, and that is exactly the part the tools now make affordable.
