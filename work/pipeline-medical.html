The situation

No tolerance for ambiguity

Pipeline Medical needed a place where healthcare procurement teams could browse, evaluate, and buy supplies and capital equipment online. There was no brand, no digital presence, and no channel to reach buyers. It had to read credible and clear for an audience that punishes ambiguity in a purchasing decision, where a moment of doubt is a lost sale.

01 / Brand identity

Clinical, credible, clean

A logo system for digital and print, a primary horizontal lockup, a vertical variant, and full-color versions. Clean and clinical, which is the right register for a B2B medical supplier whose buyers are procurement teams, not consumers.

The rebrand, from where the mark started to the system it became.
02 / Marketplace UX

A procurement workflow, not a website

The full site across three screens, a landing page, a product catalog, and a cart, designed around how procurement actually buys: clear categories, a browsable catalog, and a cart flow built for bulk and repeat orders. Around it sat Google display aimed at medical-equipment and injectable-supply buyers, social, and collateral.

The catalog organized around procurement categories, and a cart flow built for bulk and repeat orders.
03 / Ads, collateral & social

Everything the launch needed

Google display ads by product category, buyer-education one-pagers, the Capital Equipment List the procurement team worked from, and a brand social presence rounded out the launch.

Display ads by product category, buyer-education one-pagers, the Capital Equipment List, and the social header that anchored launch.
The result

Built to outlast the brief

The platform launched at supplies.pipelinemedical.com and is still live, expanded into pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, and GLP-1 medications, with distribution partners including Allergan, 3M, Molnlycke, and Provista. That it kept growing well past the original scope is the proof of the foundation the first build set. The 750,000-plus product count is the platform today, not the three-screen scope we shipped.

What it taught me

Pipeline was my early proof that good structure outlives its brief. Procurement buyers have zero tolerance for ambiguity, so every screen had to earn trust before it asked for a decision, and building it that way is why the platform is still live and scaling well past what we shipped in 2017. Designing for the foundation instead of the launch is the long-game instinct that separates a brand lead from a project designer, and it is the kind of durable thinking I want to be hired for.
