00 / The concept

A trillion-dollar category with a harm problem underneath it

Prediction markets are racing toward a trillion-dollar future, and a documented youth-gambling problem is building underneath them. Sportsbooks are pouring in, every one optimizing for time-on-app, and the people most at risk are the easiest to reach. Level is the responsible one, designed to still be the one people actually want, planted in sports, where the field is gamifying hardest. The industry figures are sourced in the research, and none of them describe this project.

01 / Positioning

Lead with responsibility, where it is hardest

The easy read was wrong. The premise that every rival looks like crypto-gambling no longer holds, because the market leader is already clean. So Level does not win on calmer visuals. It wins on a designed system of protection and legibility, and it plants that flag in sports, the most lucrative and most harmful corner. Responsible by default sets limits on from the start, an honest Starter mode for the 18 to 20 cohort, and no engagement bait. Legibility shows up as plain-language odds and an assistant that explains a market before you stake. And the system is built to grow up, category-agnostic so it extends into economics and politics without a relaunch.

02 / Identity

Level with you. Level-headed. A level playing field.

One word holds the whole stance. The mark is the name resting on a datum line, the reference a level is measured against, with a single amber index point that always sits true. Amber covers no more than a tenth of any screen, reserved for the live, the selected, and the true. The restraint is the brand, because a product that hoards its accent for what matters is one you can trust not to be playing you. Type is three voices, a warm serif for credibility, a plain sans for reading, and a monospace for every number, so the data reads like measured truth, not a scoreboard.

03 / Protection

Protection, designed as the product

The responsible-use layer is not a disclaimer, it is the product, designed first and built into every screen, and it is the senior-judgment proof. Start with friction that protects: limits on by default, raising one takes a day while lowering is instant, cooldowns after a losing run, and an understand-before-you-stake gate on unfamiliar markets. Then there is the inverse of an engagement model, the same behavioral model the category uses to maximize time-on-app, pointed the other way, watching for problem patterns and stepping in with a check-in and resources, never an upsell. And it is honest by construction, with fees and the house position shown up front, the real net on the stake screen, no streaks, and no urgency theater. Self-exclusion is one tap.

04 / The assistant

Understand it first. Then stake.

The assistant sits in front of every market. Ask it anything in plain language, and it is also the gate, with the stake button locked until you have the basics, what the number means and what it resolves on. In a category that hides the odds behind a slick interface, Level puts a teacher in front of the stake. You cannot stake on a market you have not understood, which is the whole concept in one interaction. Motion does the legibility work and carries the brand's feel, easing to rest, never flashing, the index moving last, and reduced-motion aware.

Motion that teaches and settles, easing to rest, the index moving last, the same restraint on a loss as a win.
05 / Where this goes

Where this goes

Level launches into a category projected to grow several times over by 2030, carrying a trust deficit no incumbent is addressing. The sports share of volume is forecast to fall from over 60% to about 30% by 2030, which is why Level is built to extend past sports. Category figures are sourced, and Level's own targets are projections to validate after launch, not results. It is positioned to capture three things, the responsible segment that wants in but distrusts the field, a high rate of users who clear the explainer gate before their first stake, and a trust halo with parents, regulators, and press that compounds as scrutiny on the category grows.

What it taught me

Level started as an argument with the easy version of the brief. The obvious move was calmer visuals, but the category's real problem is not how gambling looks, it is what it does, so I built the concept around protection and legibility as features, not warnings. Designing restraint on purpose taught me that a point of view is worth more than a nicer interface, and that is the judgment I want speculative work like this to show, not just the craft.
