The Brief

Why this exists

One person, two audiences. Hiring managers and brand leadership scan fast for range and seniority, while design-literate viewers judge taste and craft. The system had to land a strong first impression and hold up on the second and third look.

Most people keep three things apart: design taste, systems thinking, and marketing accountability. I don't. I started in code and moved to design, so I think in systems first, but the system I care about most is the person on the other end. The job of the brand is simple: make one connected practice read as one thing, not a stack of separate skills. This page is the proof, not the claim, a reference you could build against, and a case study of the thinking behind it.

Where it comes from

I grew up in coding camps and assumed I'd follow my dad into programming. In 2016 I started volunteering on a nonprofit's design and social team, and that turned the career around. The engineering never left. It became how I build brands: I build the system that makes the work, so every run after takes less effort.

None of this is theory. The practice it documents is the one that ran SPORTIME's marketing, where a video-first program drove 11.7M cross-platform impressions and nearly 17,000 new followers in a year.

The two-accent rule

The accents never cross. Teal carries identity and interaction; coral marks the annotation layer. This keeps both meaningful instead of decorative, and it is the rule that makes the palette feel designed rather than busy.

Teal · identity & interaction

  • Links, hovers, and focus states
  • Filters and active nav
  • The marquee
  • Heading accent words
  • Result-box outline

Coral · annotation layer

  • Project years
  • Section index numbers
  • Experience & chapter numbers
  • Result labels
  • Availability dot, footer CTA hover

No pure white or pure black anywhere. Accents never fill large areas or set body copy, the one exception being the marquee, which is deliberately loud.

One real constraint: Slate on Graphite lands at 4.48:1, just under the 4.5 AA line for normal text. On cards, set normal-size secondary text in Mono #CFCFCF, and reserve Slate for labels at 19px bold and up, where AA Large applies.

Cover & gradient treatment

Hero and card covers use abstract teal-on-ink backgrounds when no single still earns the space. Over any cover, titles sit on a fixed legibility wash: a top-to-bottom gradient from rgba(10,10,10,.72) to .92 at 65% and into solid Ink, so type stays readable no matter the image behind it.

Light contexts

The brand is dark-native. Where a surface must be light, like the resume or print, the system inverts: Ink #0A0A0A text on Bone #FAFAFA, the same three typefaces, and the same two-accent rule. One change is deliberate and required. The dark-native accents are too light to read on a light ground (Electric Teal measures 1.6:1 on Bone, Coral 2.6:1, both under AA), so light contexts use a darker, verified pair: Teal Deep #0A8071 and Coral Deep #D03C21, each clearing AA at 4.5:1 or better for normal text. This is the one place the accents shift value, and it is a defined variation for the resume and print, documented so it reads as intentional, not a third accent added to fill the gap.

The mark

Aperture mark · favicon, app icon

The aperture reads as a lens iris and a half-disc: coral outer arc, teal body, ink pupil, the two accents in their fixed roles. It is the only illustrated mark in the system and exists for sizes and surfaces where the wordmark cannot be read, never as a replacement for the name in normal use.

Role descriptor

One of three exact forms, never invented variants: Creative & Marketing Strategist / Creative Strategist / Social & Digital Marketing Lead. Clear space around the wordmark equals the cap-height of the name. Minimum 16px cap-height so it stays legible in the nav.

Misuse

Do

  • Keep the wordmark in the display face, uppercase
  • Recolor only the © (teal) and the full mark in Ink or Bone
  • Give it clear space equal to the cap-height
  • Use the aperture only at favicon and app-icon scale

Avoid

  • Stretching, condensing, or re-weighting the name
  • Recoloring the wordmark in teal or coral
  • Drop shadows, outlines, gradients, or effects
  • Filling the aperture pupil or swapping its accent roles
  • Setting it over a busy photo without the legibility wash

Voice

How it reads

The writing carries as much of the brand as the visuals. The house voice is an operator with a point of view. Lead with an earned belief, then prove it with real work, in concrete images and honest numbers, one quotable line per moment. Outcome first, technically fluent, no preamble. If a sentence is not adding information, cut it.

Do

  • Lead with an earned belief, then prove it
  • Lead with the outcome, then the process
  • Use specific, verified numbers
  • Run creative and marketing as one practice
  • Match tone to the audience and seniority

Avoid

  • Fabricated metrics, titles, or credentials
  • Generic filler: unlock, elevate, seamless, passionate
  • Clipped, choppy cadence, or sentences that sound assembled
  • Over-formal resume-speak and stacked adjectives

Tone, pinned

One voice, the tone flexes by page. On the four tone dimensions the house voice sits at serious with occasional dry wit, casual and direct, lightly irreverent, and enthusiastic but earned. Warmest on the home and contact pages, plainer on a case study, most opinionated in the journal.

Words it reaches forPlainspoken, concrete, outcome-first

One message, three registers

The same idea, "I run creative and marketing together, on the same project," tuned to who is reading. Tone shifts; the facts never do.

Fintech CFO

Creative and performance owned by one person means less rework and a faster path from brief to launch, and every decision ties back to a number you can track.

Sports-venue brand

The look has to hit from the upper deck and the feed, the same energy on a jumbotron and a phone. I build the identity and the motion and the social engine together so game day feels like one thing, everywhere fans see it.

Hiring manager

I run social, design, UI/UX, and performance on the same project instead of handing them off. The CS degree is why I think in systems and measure like a marketer. The portfolio shows the range; the SPORTIME results are attached.

Naming stays consistent too: role descriptor uses the three approved forms only, and project and file names follow the site's lowercase-kebab convention.

(10)

Method

How it is made

How the work is made, because the process is part of the brand. The order is deliberate: audience first, organization second, channel never in isolation.

I start at the audience level, what the person on the other end already knows and responds to, and the organization's needs come in after that, never before. You can usually tell when work was built inside-out. From the first sketch I concept cross-channel, social, design, UI/UX, motion, and performance considered together rather than bolted on after.
