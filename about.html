Audience first. Systems always.

I was supposed to be a programmer. I grew up in coding camps, studied computer science at NYU, and figured I'd end up on the same path as my dad. Then in 2016 I started helping a nonprofit's marketing and design team build out its US division, and the plan changed. For the first time I was making things people actually responded to, not just things that ran, and I didn't want to go back. So I didn't. Agency internships, an agency seat, in-house, then startups, each step further in.

The engineering brain didn't leave. It just aimed at a different problem.

The audience is the system

I still think in systems first, but the system I care about most is the person on the other end. Before anything gets designed or built, I want to know what they already know, what they need, and what actually moves them. Everything after that is execution, and you can usually tell when work was built the other way around.

One idea, all the way through

I never picked a lane. Brand, motion, UI/UX, social, paid, I'd rather take one idea all the way through than hand it off four times and watch it lose the thread. If there's a throughline, it's motion. I think video is where a brand should start, not what it upgrades to, and the work that's done the most for me has almost always been the stuff that moves. Lately that means building Gen AI into the production itself, turning the repeatable, multi-step setups that used to eat a team's week into something one person runs in a day, and the seams don't show.

Build it right once so it runs cleaner every time after, ideally without me in the room.

Off the clock

None of this switches off when I leave the desk. I'm wired to set things up right the first time, because anything I'll do more than once should cost less effort the next time, not the same again. My coffee is the clearest tell: same build every morning and again at four, milk and sugar in before it brews so it mixes on the way down and the only thing left to do is stir. The kitchen runs the same way, every ingredient out and in order before I start, a habit drilled in young when my mom taught me to cook, so the dish lands the way I like it every time. Even a Yankees game is mostly me reading the matchup and calling the pitch before it's thrown, for the satisfaction of being right.

The other half of me is endlessly, uselessly curious, ancient history, architecture, cars, space, anime, coffee, whatever's in reach, to the point that friends have stopped asking why I know any of it. The one creative thing I haven't built yet is music. I've been singing since I was a kid, and writing an album is the long game, the project I'm quietly saving all this range for. Same instinct as the rest of it: figure out how something works, set it up to run clean, and never have to solve it twice.
